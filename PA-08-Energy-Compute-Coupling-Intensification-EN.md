## 1. Prediction Statement



During the Pre-ASI Super Prosperity Phase described in C06-SPP, the continuing demand of AI systems for compute will intensify the coupling between energy systems and compute infrastructure.



This coupling will not merely appear as rising electricity consumption by data centers. It will increasingly appear as a structural linkage among compute facilities, power supply, grid planning, energy investment, cooling systems, storage systems, and geographic site selection.



As AI model training, inference, automated agents, robotics, and physical infrastructure management continue to expand, energy will no longer function merely as an external input to AI infrastructure. It will become one of the core constraints on compute growth.



Therefore, future AI competition will increasingly become a combined competition over compute and energy. Regions with stable, low-cost, scalable, and governable energy systems will acquire higher strategic value in the AI infrastructure race.



---



## 2. Theoretical Mapping



PA-08 corresponds directly to the Compute-Energy-Infrastructure Center mechanism in C06-SPP.



In the IEH framework, AI is a special information system whose expansion does not occur only at the software layer. As AI capability grows, its demand spills over into physical infrastructure: compute, energy, grids, cooling, chips, land, minerals, and automated maintenance systems.



PA-08 focuses on one core mechanism:



> Compute growth increasingly converts into pressure on energy systems, and energy systems are increasingly reorganized around AI infrastructure demand.



This means that energy demand in the AI age should not be understood merely as a continuation of traditional industrial electricity demand. It is a new structural demand generated by the expansion of advanced information systems into the physical world.



---



## 3. Mechanism



The mechanism behind PA-08 can be summarized as follows:



1. AI model capability improves;

2. Training and inference demand expand;

3. GPU, ASIC, and data center scale increase;

4. Electricity, cooling, and grid capacity become key constraints;

5. Data center location becomes increasingly dependent on energy conditions;

6. Energy planning begins to incorporate AI infrastructure demand;

7. Compute infrastructure and energy infrastructure become structurally coupled;

8. Regional competitive advantage expands from software, capital, and talent to power, grids, and energy governance capacity.



This mechanism does not require AI to already be fully autonomous, nor does it require ASI to have emerged. It occurs during the Pre-ASI stage as part of the physical infrastructure expansion of the Super Prosperity Phase.



---



## 4. Observable Indicators



PA-08 can be evaluated through the following observable indicators:



1. Major technology companies and AI companies directly investing in electricity generation, nuclear energy, renewable energy, energy storage, or grid-related projects;

2. Data center location decisions increasingly emphasizing power availability, low-carbon electricity, cooling conditions, and grid access;

3. Government energy planning, regional industrial policy, and grid investment documents explicitly incorporating AI data center and compute infrastructure demand;

4. Utilities and energy infrastructure companies identifying AI data centers as a long-term demand source;

5. Data center projects facing approval constraints due to grid capacity, electricity prices, water resources, cooling conditions, or community capacity;

6. AI infrastructure investment spilling over from data center construction into generation, transmission, storage, cooling, and automated maintenance systems;

7. Policy discussions increasingly linking compute sovereignty, energy sovereignty, and data sovereignty;

8. Regions with stable low-cost energy and large-scale land resources becoming more strategically attractive for AI infrastructure investment.



---



## 5. Falsification Conditions



PA-08 does not claim that all AI infrastructure will expand indefinitely. It also does not claim that every data center investment is economically rational.



The prediction should be weakened or revised if the following conditions persist:



1. AI model and inference efficiency improve so rapidly that aggregate energy demand remains low over the long term;

2. Data center energy consumption does not become an important variable in grid planning, energy investment, or policy debate;

3. AI infrastructure investment remains limited to ordinary commercial real estate or local cloud expansion, without triggering structural responses in energy systems;

4. Major technology companies and governments do not treat energy, grids, and compute as a combined strategic issue;

5. Compute competition is primarily resolved by algorithmic efficiency rather than energy and infrastructure expansion.



If these conditions persist over time, the Energy–Compute Coupling Intensification mechanism proposed by PA-08 would not hold or would need to be significantly downgraded.

## 7. Strategic Implications



If PA-08 holds, AI competition will no longer be only a competition over models, algorithms, talent, and capital. It will also become a competition over energy systems, infrastructure planning, and national organizational capacity.



This implies that:



1. Countries rich in energy resources but weak in organizational capacity may not automatically gain advantage in the AI age;

2. Compute infrastructure will increasingly depend on long-term energy planning rather than short-term market procurement;

3. Grid policy, cooling policy, storage policy, and land-use policy will become part of AI industrial policy;

4. Data centers should not be treated merely as ordinary commercial projects, but as compute-energy coupling nodes;

5. AI infrastructure governance will become an important component of national competition in the Pre-ASI stage.



In the IEH framework, this trend shows that the expansion of advanced information systems projects their requirements into the physical world and forces societies to reorganize energy, infrastructure, and governance structures.
