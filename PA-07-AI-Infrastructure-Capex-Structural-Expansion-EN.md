## 1. Prediction Statement

This prediction argues that the growth of AI infrastructure capital expenditure should not be understood merely as a short-term investment boom within the technology sector. Nor should it be understood only as a temporary phase in which large cloud providers expand data centers or purchase GPUs.

In the Pre-ASI stage described by C06-SPP, the improvement of AI capability will gradually transform AI’s own development requirements into society-level capital allocation demand.

This capital allocation demand will not remain confined to model training, cloud services, and data centers. It will structurally spill over into a broader set of material foundation systems, including:

- Power;
- Grids;
- Chips;
- Cooling;
- Land;
- Water resources;
- Communication networks;
- Critical minerals;
- Automated manufacturing;
- Robotics systems;
- Data center operations;
- Physical maintenance systems;
- Energy and industrial infrastructure.

Therefore, the core prediction of PA-07 is not simply that “AI infrastructure investment will increase.”

More precisely, this prediction asks:

> **Will AI infrastructure capital expenditure evolve from the current boom into a long-term material foundation expansion cycle that cuts across industries, energy systems, manufacturing systems, and national strategy?**

The core proposition of this prediction is:

> **During the Pre-ASI Super Prosperity Phase described by C06-SPP, AI infrastructure capital expenditure will not remain a short-term GPU or data center investment wave. It will structurally spill over into power, grids, chips, cooling, land, minerals, automated manufacturing, robotics systems, and physical maintenance systems, and gradually become an important component of macro fixed asset investment, energy planning, and national industrial policy.**

This expansion should not be understood as an ordinary technology-sector business cycle.

It is closer to a new process of material foundation reorganization:

1. AI capability improves;
2. AI systems’ own material demand increases;
3. Firms and capital markets increase investment in AI infrastructure;
4. Energy, grids, chips, cooling, manufacturing, and robotics systems are mobilized;
5. AI obtains a stronger material foundation;
6. AI capability improves further;
7. A new round of infrastructure capital expenditure expansion begins.

Therefore, PA-07 predicts that AI will gradually shift from being a software-sector variable to becoming a macro fixed asset investment variable, an energy-system variable, an industrial-policy variable, and a national-strategy variable.

## 3. Mechanism

The mechanism of PA-07 can be divided into six layers.

---

### 3.1 AI Shifts from a Software System to an Infrastructure System

Early AI can easily be understood as software, models, algorithms, or internet applications.

However, as AI capability continues to improve, especially with the expansion of large-scale model training, inference deployment, agent systems, automated programming, robotic control, and physical-world execution systems, AI will become increasingly dependent on underlying physical infrastructure.

These infrastructures include:

- High-performance chips;
- Data centers;
- Power systems;
- Cooling systems;
- Fiber and network connectivity;
- Cloud computing platforms;
- Automated manufacturing facilities;
- Robotics systems;
- Maintenance systems;
- Resource and logistics systems.

Therefore, AI is not merely a software-sector variable. It will become an infrastructure capital expenditure variable.

---

### 3.2 AI’s Own Development Becomes a New Social Demand

Traditional technologies mainly provide tools around pre-existing human needs.

The special feature of AI is that the improvement of AI’s own capability is increasingly treated by firms, states, capital markets, and security systems as a new core demand.

This demand is not the demand of a single consumer. It is a system-level demand:

- Firms need AI to improve efficiency;
- Capital needs AI to generate returns;
- States need AI to gain strategic advantage;
- Military and security systems need AI to improve capability;
- Scientific research systems need AI to accelerate discovery;
- AI systems themselves need more compute and material foundations.

Therefore, the development demand of AI will be transformed into society-level capital allocation demand.

Within the IEH framework, this means that AI is not only a tool for satisfying human needs. It is also beginning to become a special technological system capable of continuously generating its own material demand.

---

### 3.3 Capital Expenditure Spills Over from the Model Layer to the Physical Layer

The improvement of AI capability first drives the expansion of compute demand.

The expansion of compute demand further drives:

1. Model training demand;
2. GPU / AI accelerator procurement;
3. Data center construction;
4. Rising electricity demand;
5. Grid expansion;
6. Cooling system upgrades;
7. Rising demand for land, water resources, and energy contracts;
8. Expansion of chip manufacturing and server supply chains;
9. Expansion of automated manufacturing and robotics systems.

This means that AI capital expenditure will not remain at the GPU or model training layer. It will spill over into the broader physical infrastructure layer.

The key observation point of PA-07 is precisely whether this spillover continues to occur.

---

### 3.4 AI CapEx Becomes a Macro Variable Instead of a Technology-Sector Variable

If AI infrastructure capital expenditure remains only within the financial statements of a few major technology companies, it can still be understood as an internal technology-sector cycle.

But if it continues to affect the following fields:

- Grid planning;
- Energy investment;
- Semiconductor manufacturing;
- Land use;
- Water resource management;
- Industrial park construction;
- Critical mineral policy;
- Robotics and automation industries;
- National industrial strategy;
- Local fiscal systems and regional economies;

then AI CapEx will no longer be merely an investment behavior by technology companies. It will become a macroeconomic variable.

This is the core incremental judgment of PA-07 compared with ordinary market observation.

---

### 3.5 Capital Markets Reinforce the Positive Feedback Loop

If markets continue to expect AI to bring higher productivity, greater capital returns, and stronger strategic advantage, capital markets will continue to finance AI infrastructure expansion.

This capital input will then improve AI’s material foundation, further advancing model capability, application capability, and automation capability.

Therefore, capital markets will become a key amplifier in the C06-SPP positive feedback loop.

This mechanism can be summarized as:

1. AI capability progresses;
2. Market expectations rise;
3. Financing and capital expenditure increase;
4. Infrastructure expands;
5. AI capability improves further;
6. Market expectations rise further.

However, this positive feedback is not risk-free.

If AI application revenue, productivity improvement, or strategic returns fall short of expectations, capital markets may weaken or even interrupt the cycle.

Therefore, the sustained confidence of capital markets is an important observation variable for PA-07.

---

### 3.6 National Strategy Further Reinforces Infrastructure Investment

As AI is treated as a core variable in national competition, industrial security, military capability, and technological sovereignty, national-level actors may also continue to promote AI infrastructure investment.

This includes:

- National AI infrastructure strategies;
- Support for data centers and cloud infrastructure;
- Subsidies for chip manufacturing and advanced packaging;
- Grid and energy investment;
- Critical mineral strategies;
- Robotics and automation industrial policy;
- Public compute platforms;
- Sovereign cloud;
- Defense and security AI infrastructure.

Therefore, PA-07 is not only a prediction about corporate investment. It also includes a prediction about national-level infrastructure investment.

If AI infrastructure is increasingly treated by more states as strategic infrastructure, the strength of PA-07 will increase significantly.

## 5. Verification Criteria

The verification of PA-07 should not be based only on whether “AI CapEx increases.” It should instead observe whether AI CapEx develops structural, cross-industry, and long-duration features.

---

### 5.1 Weak Signal

The following conditions constitute weak signals:

- Major technology companies continue to raise AI-related capital expenditure guidance;
- Data centers, power, chips, and cooling become core topics in AI industry discussion;
- Multiple countries begin discussing AI infrastructure strategies;
- Markets begin to understand AI not only as model competition but also as infrastructure competition;
- AI infrastructure / compute / data center capex is repeatedly emphasized in corporate financial disclosures.

---

### 5.2 Medium Signal

The following conditions constitute medium signals:

- AI data center investment grows significantly for multiple consecutive years;
- The share of AI infrastructure in cloud providers’ capital expenditure continues to rise;
- Utilities, energy companies, and data center companies become visibly linked;
- AI infrastructure investment produces observable effects on regional economic growth, land prices, grid planning, and industrial policy;
- Chips, energy, grids, cooling, and data centers are jointly treated as the core AI industrial chain;
- AI infrastructure investment begins to affect public policy, regulatory approvals, and national industrial strategy.

---

### 5.3 Strong Signal

The following conditions constitute strong signals:

- AI infrastructure capital expenditure becomes an important new category within global fixed asset investment;
- Multiple countries incorporate AI data centers, compute, power, and chip systems into national strategic infrastructure;
- AI infrastructure investment significantly affects macroeconomics, energy planning, industrial policy, and capital market valuations;
- AI capital expenditure expansion forms a cross-industry long-term cycle rather than a short-term boom within a single technology sector;
- Data centers, grids, chips, cooling, automated manufacturing, and robotics systems jointly form a statistically identifiable AI material foundation expansion chain;
- AI infrastructure expansion begins to be treated as the core material foundation of the Pre-ASI stage.

## 6. Falsification Conditions

The following conditions would weaken or partially falsify PA-07.

---

### 6.1 AI CapEx Remains Only a Short-Term GPU / Data Center Investment Boom

If AI infrastructure investment remains mainly concentrated in GPU procurement and data center expansion by a small number of cloud providers, without significantly spilling over into power, grids, cooling, chip manufacturing, minerals, automated manufacturing, and robotics systems, then the structural expansion claim of PA-07 would be weakened.

---

### 6.2 AI Infrastructure Investment Clearly Falls After 2027–2029

If AI infrastructure capital expenditure clearly declines after a short-term peak and is shown to have been mainly a one-time overinvestment or bubble cycle, then PA-07’s claim about a long-term capital expenditure cycle would be weakened.

---

### 6.3 AI’s Economic Impact Remains Long-Term Light-Asset Software

If the main economic impact of AI remains confined to software subscriptions, application-layer efficiency gains, and light-asset services, without driving heavy-asset infrastructure expansion, then PA-07 would be weakened.

---

### 6.4 Compute Efficiency Offsets the Need for Infrastructure Expansion

If model architectures, chip efficiency, algorithmic efficiency, or distributed computing methods significantly reduce the need for data center, power, and hardware expansion, then AI infrastructure capital expenditure may not enter a long-term structural expansion cycle.

---

### 6.5 Capital Markets Lose Confidence in AI Investment Returns

If AI application returns are significantly lower than expected, causing capital markets to sharply reduce AI infrastructure investment, then the positive feedback mechanism of PA-07 would be weakened.

---

### 6.6 Policy, Energy, or Social Backlash Suppresses Expansion

If grid bottlenecks, environmental pressure, community backlash, energy prices, regulatory limits, or national security policies suppress AI infrastructure construction over the long term, then the expansion path of PA-07 may be significantly weakened or delayed.

---

### 6.7 National Strategy Does Not Absorb the AI Infrastructure Issue

If most countries do not incorporate AI data centers, compute, power, chips, and infrastructure into strategic infrastructure or industrial policy frameworks, and instead treat them merely as ordinary commercial investments, then PA-07’s national-strategic claim would be weakened.

---

## 7. Exclusion and Revision Conditions

The following conditions do not directly falsify PA-07, but they require revisions to the interpretation of the prediction.

---

### 7.1 Geographic Relocation

If AI infrastructure capital expenditure does not occur in some expected regions but relocates to other regions with more suitable energy, land, or institutional conditions, this indicates that capital expenditure expansion still holds, while its geographic distribution needs revision.

---

### 7.2 Technological Path Change

If future AI computing shifts from centralized data centers to edge computing, distributed computing, specialized chips, or new computing architectures, the type of infrastructure must be reassessed, but this does not necessarily negate capital expenditure expansion.

---

### 7.3 Cyclical Correction

If AI capital expenditure experiences a short-term bubble, correction, or industry consolidation, but the long-term trend remains upward, this does not constitute falsification.

---

### 7.4 Statistical Classification Changes

If capital expenditure is classified under cloud computing, grids, energy, semiconductors, automation, or industrial investment, rather than directly labeled as AI investment, the statistical classification should be explained in the evidence log.

---

### 7.5 Change in Investment Actors

If capital expenditure shifts from large technology companies to utilities, infrastructure funds, sovereign wealth funds, industrial companies, or government platforms, this indicates a change in investment actors, but it does not necessarily weaken PA-07.
